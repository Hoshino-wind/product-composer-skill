# Taste Calibration

Use this to prevent technically correct UI from becoming ugly, generic, over-explained, or visually timid.

## Taste Principle

Taste is not a style. Taste is the ability to choose, refuse, and make a product feel inevitable.

Good taste in UI usually comes from:

- one clear aesthetic stance
- fewer but stronger forms
- proportion before ornament
- visual tension without noise
- specific details instead of generic polish
- confidence to leave things out

## Taste Brief

Before producing an important visual direction, write:

- Taste stance: three words, for example `precise, quiet, charged`.
- Anti-reference: three unwanted associations, for example `dashboard template`, `startup gradient`, `enterprise flowchart`.
- Signature form: the one shape, object, or spatial gesture that carries the design.
- Signature restraint: what the design refuses to show.
- Tension pair: two forces that create interest, for example `warm paper vs cold instrument`, `editorial air vs operational control`, `soft surface vs sharp state`.
- Deletion rule: the first thing to remove when the design becomes busy.

## Taste Axes

Choose positions deliberately:

- Sparse vs dense
- Editorial vs operational
- Warm vs clinical
- Tactile vs purely digital
- Calm vs charged
- Familiar vs frontier
- Flat vs dimensional
- Systematic vs expressive

Do not sit in the middle of every axis. Blandness often comes from refusing to choose.

## Curation Loop

1. Start with the strongest single idea.
2. Remove every secondary idea that weakens it.
3. Make the silhouette work before adding detail.
4. Make the palette work with three roles: base, text, accent.
5. Make typography carry hierarchy before adding boxes.
6. Add one signature detail, not five.
7. Squint-test the design: if it becomes a gray mush, rebuild the composition.
8. Blur-test the design: if the hierarchy disappears, simplify.
9. Thumbnail-test the design: if it has no memorable contour, strengthen the dominant form.

## Common Taste Failures

- Concept dump: every idea is visible at once.
- UI kit collage: components are arranged but not composed.
- Fake premium: beige background, thin borders, and soft shadows without a real idea.
- Fake futurism: glow, glass, particles, and dark panels without a control model.
- Enterprise diagram: the interface explains the workflow but has no visual soul.
- Safe neutrality: everything is technically acceptable and emotionally dead.
- Over-labeling: labels compensate for weak shape, grouping, and hierarchy.
- Decorative novelty: the new look does not improve control, meaning, or memory.

## Better Moves

- Replace many panels with one memorable instrument.
- Replace equal boxes with a dominant object and attached satellites.
- Replace explanation with direct manipulation affordances.
- Replace decorative color with state-bearing color.
- Replace generic cards with spatial layers.
- Replace symmetrical balance with purposeful asymmetry.
- Replace more details with sharper hierarchy.

## Critique Rubric

Score mentally from 1 to 5:

- Fit: does the aesthetic match the product's job?
- Form: is there a dominant visual object or spatial idea?
- Proportion: do scale, rhythm, and negative space feel intentional?
- Specificity: could this belong only to this product?
- Restraint: did the design refuse enough?
- Tension: is there tasteful contrast without chaos?
- Craft: do typography, color, state, and spacing feel resolved?
- Memory: what does the viewer remember after five seconds?

If `Form`, `Proportion`, or `Memory` is weak, do not polish details. Redesign the composition.

## Image Prompt Guidance

When generating UI images:

- Ask for `design-board quality`, not a literal app screenshot full of tiny labels.
- Specify the taste stance and anti-reference.
- Specify one signature form.
- Ask for sparse labels and strong negative space.
- Ask for the image to read well as a black-and-white thumbnail.
- Avoid asking the model to show every feature or every interaction primitive.

Good prompt fragment:

```text
Taste stance: precise, quiet, charged.
Anti-reference: no dashboard template, no startup gradient, no enterprise flowchart.
Dominant form: one tactile mission instrument that carries the whole composition.
Signature restraint: most system state is implied through form and behavior, not shown as panels.
```
