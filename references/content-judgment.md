# Content Judgment

Use this when a UI feels dense, crowded, over-explained, or assembled from too many valid ideas.

## Principle

Design is deciding what not to show yet.

A first viewport should not prove every capability. It should create a clear mental model, one desire to continue, and one reason to act.

## First-Viewport Budget

For most product homepages, start with:

- one headline
- one supporting sentence
- one primary action
- one secondary action at most
- one product proof moment
- one quiet next-section hint

Everything else must earn its place.

## Keep / Defer / Delete

Before designing, make a decision table:

- `Keep`: required for five-second understanding.
- `Defer`: useful, but belongs below the fold, in a tab, hover, modal, detail page, or later sequence.
- `Delete`: true but not important, redundant, self-congratulatory, or included only to show effort.

If two things communicate the same value, keep the more visual one and delete the more verbose one.

## Density Rules

- Use at most three competing content zones in the first viewport.
- Avoid showing process, proof, critique, examples, references, scores, and CTAs all at once.
- Do not expose internal methodology unless it helps the buyer/user decide.
- Prefer one strong product proof over many small panels.
- Use below-the-fold hints for breadth.
- Let whitespace carry confidence.
- Keep one curiosity gap unresolved when it increases viewing desire without harming clarity.

## Common Overload Failures

- Showing the entire workflow in the hero.
- Showing both before/after and scoring and critique and references at once.
- Turning proof into a dashboard.
- Adding a bottom strip because the top was not decisive.
- Using labels to compensate for weak visual hierarchy.
- Treating every capability as equal.

## Better Moves

- Compress a workflow into one decisive moment.
- Replace multiple scores with one visible quality verdict.
- Replace four cards with one before/after comparison.
- Replace explanatory sidebars with a small annotation.
- Move secondary proof below the fold.
- Cut the nav if the homepage needs more breathing room.
- Replace neutral explanation with one transformation the user wants to experience.

## Acceptance Check

The page passes if a viewer can answer these in five seconds:

- What is this?
- Why should I care?
- What proof did I see?
- What can I do next?

If the viewer starts reading small panels to understand the page, the design is too dense.
