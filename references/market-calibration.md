# Market Calibration

Use this when a homepage feels fake, self-indulgent, over-designed, or disconnected from real product value.

## Principle

Excellent product websites do not just look polished. They create immediate belief.

They usually combine:

- sharp positioning
- visible product proof
- strong hierarchy
- restrained brand behavior
- narrative momentum
- obvious next action

Market calibration is not the same as copying common SaaS composition. A page can contain product proof and still feel generic if it uses the default formula.

## What Good Sites Do

### 1. Lead With Positioning

The headline is not decorative. It tells users what category the product changes.

Good pattern:
- short claim
- specific product category
- explicit audience or job
- no generic "beautiful, powerful, seamless" filler

### 2. Show Product Evidence Early

The hero visual should prove the product, not symbolize it.

Use:
- real interface preview
- realistic product state
- interactive demo frame
- short workflow sequence
- concrete before/after
- customer proof or metric

Avoid:
- abstract 3D object
- fake device render
- decorative dashboard collage
- metaphor object with no product evidence

### 3. Build A Narrative, Not A Poster

The first viewport should create a reason to scroll.

Structure:
- headline: what changed
- subcopy: why it matters
- product proof: how it works
- CTA: what to try next
- next-section hint: what evidence comes next

### 4. Make The Brand System Behave

Brand is not just logo, palette, and hero object.

It shows up in:
- motion logic
- interaction states
- component rhythm
- icon style
- typography behavior
- how examples are written
- how proof is staged

### 5. Use Proof Instead Of Vibes

Credibility can come from:
- customer logos
- concrete metrics
- named workflows
- realistic example data
- recognizable integrations
- product screenshots that look operational
- short demo clips or frames

## Calibration Questions

Before accepting a homepage:

- What do I know about the product after five seconds?
- What evidence did I see?
- What makes this product different from a template?
- Is the hero visual proving the product or just decorating the page?
- Would the page still work if the art direction were toned down?
- Is there a next step I understand immediately?
- Does the composition feel selected, or merely assembled from a landing page pattern?
- Has the page deferred enough useful detail to feel confident rather than crowded?

## Template Formula To Avoid

Do not treat this as the default shape for all product websites:

- nav bar
- left headline and subcopy
- two CTAs
- right floating product screenshot
- bottom three proof cards

This layout is acceptable only when the product, copy, motion, and art direction make it feel inevitable. Otherwise it reads like a template.

Other formulaic patterns to distrust:

- centered mega headline plus abstract product glow
- screenshot collage without a clear workflow
- three equal feature cards directly under the hero
- fake customer logos without context
- "AI for X" headline with generic dashboard mock
- decorative 3D object beside vague promise
- large rounded browser frame with no memorable product state

## Direction Exploration

For weak or template-like homepages, generate or propose multiple visual directions before committing:

1. **Editorial System**: strong typography, asymmetric proof, restrained product fragments, sharp copy.
2. **Product Cinema**: one real workflow staged as a sequence, like a paused demo with tension.
3. **Tool Surface**: the page itself behaves like the product, with controls and state embedded in the hero.
4. **Manifesto + Evidence**: bold point of view paired with compact proof, not a decorative poster.
5. **Reference Desk**: dense but beautiful knowledge/work surface, closer to a crafted editorial tool than SaaS marketing.

Pick one direction based on fit, not novelty.

## Theme Marketplace Calibration

Use this when the user points to ThemeForest, WordPress themes, Elementor templates, landing page marketplaces, Webflow templates, or commercial website template galleries.

The useful lesson is commercial maturity, not copying template skins. Theme marketplaces optimize for immediate buyer confidence: a page must show category fit, polished section rhythm, visible demos, and conversion evidence quickly.

Observed marketplace signals:

- Vertical specificity: themes are organized by real buyer categories such as corporate, creative, retail, eCommerce, blog/magazine, technology, nonprofit, education, real estate, wedding, and mobile.
- Demo-first proof: theme cards and pages depend on previewability. A visitor should immediately understand what the finished site will look like and where to click next.
- Market proof: price, ratings, sales counts, bestseller labels, new releases, and category placement create credibility.
- Multipurpose vs niche routes: bestsellers often sell breadth and builder flexibility, while modern niche templates sell a sharp industry scene.
- Section completeness: strong templates usually include hero, category/value blocks, featured demos, product/service proof, testimonials/logos, pricing or offer, blog/resource, and footer conversion.
- Visual packaging: polished thumbnails, device mockups, demo grids, badges, sale labels, and consistent card rhythm make the template feel ready to use.

What to borrow:

- Make the first viewport clearly identify the business category and buyer promise.
- Stage a realistic finished-site preview, not only an abstract product mockup.
- Use modular section rhythm so the page feels complete beyond the hero.
- Include trust proof early: demos, usage examples, customer logos, sales/ratings-style proof, integrations, or case results.
- Let visual style come from the industry: jewelry, SaaS, agency, architecture, real estate, restaurant, education, healthcare, travel, or retail should not share the same palette and section rhythm.
- Give the page a strong commercial path: preview, compare, choose, book, buy, contact, or start.

What to avoid:

- Copying exact marketplace templates, names, layouts, thumbnails, or demo compositions.
- Generic "multipurpose" pages with no actual industry point of view.
- Overstuffed demo grids that make a custom product feel like a template catalog.
- Polished hero images that do not show an actual offer, product, venue, service, or workflow.

Prompt fragment:

```text
Theme marketplace calibration: commercial WordPress-template maturity without copying a template. Strong category fit, polished first viewport, realistic finished-site proof, modular section rhythm, early trust signals, clear preview/booking/purchase path, and industry-specific visual language. Avoid generic multipurpose demo grids, copied marketplace layout, and decorative hero visuals with no offer proof.
```

## Redesign Moves

If the homepage is ugly:

- Remove the metaphor object.
- Replace it with a focused product preview.
- Replace formulaic left/right SaaS layout with a more specific composition.
- Cut the first viewport to one message, one proof moment, and one action path.
- Make the headline sharper and less poetic.
- Move from "look at our aesthetic" to "see the product working."
- Use one real workflow example.
- Add quiet proof below the fold.
- Reduce labels and decorative controls.

## Prompt Guidance

When generating UI mockups:

- Ask for a homepage built around a real product preview.
- Specify the exact product state shown in the hero.
- Include one short proof strip below the fold.
- Avoid standalone abstract hero objects unless they directly demonstrate interaction.
- Ask for a site that could plausibly ship, not a concept poster.
