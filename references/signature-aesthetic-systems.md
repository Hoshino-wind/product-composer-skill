# Signature Aesthetic Systems

Use this when a UI needs the kind of coherence, beauty, and memorability that a strong museum, cultural, editorial, hospitality, premium brand, or product launch page requires. The goal is to build a reusable visual system, not to copy a style.

## Core Principle

A beautiful page usually works because every visible choice belongs to one world. Layout, image, color, typography, section transition, icon style, and content density all point to the same source.

The Dunhuang-style result worked because it behaved like a complete visual world:

- The cave opening created a natural stage and frame.
- Ceiling patterns, wall paintings, ribbons, desert light, towers, and artifacts came from the same cultural source.
- Warm mineral colors and muted teal/red accents were specific to the subject.
- The scroll edge connected the hero to lower sections.
- The first screen had one emotional scene, then the next content appeared as a continuation rather than a separate card grid.
- Navigation and CTAs were present, but visually subordinate to the world.

Do not reuse these Dunhuang motifs unless the subject is actually Dunhuang or a related cultural theme. Reuse the structure of the thinking.

## Signature System Recipe

Before visual generation or implementation, define:

1. Subject material: real motifs, materials, artifacts, places, product surfaces, or states that make this subject recognizable.
2. Dominant scene: one memorable spatial idea such as a cave, chamber, stage, atlas, table, corridor, horizon, instrument, archive, or portrait.
3. Motif hierarchy: one large framing motif, one movement motif, one detail motif, and one UI motif.
4. Palette source: colors from physical material, product state, environment, or brand asset.
5. Texture rule: paper, stone, silk, metal, ceramic, glass, native software, or clean print; choose one primary material behavior.
6. Section continuity: how the first, second, and third screens connect through edge treatment, rhythm, object trail, light path, or repeated motif.
7. UI assimilation: controls, cards, labels, and navigation should feel born from the system, not pasted on top.
8. Silence budget: decide which areas must stay quiet so the signature form can breathe.

## Page Types

### Cultural / Museum

- Use a place-based spatial metaphor: gallery, archive, cave, courtyard, map, manuscript, stage, route, or ritual path.
- Let artifacts and environment carry emotion; keep UI controls thin, restrained, and archival.
- Use section transitions as narrative movement, not hard page breaks.
- Avoid generic card decks, tourist-poster collage, and over-labeled history timelines.

### Product / SaaS

- Use a product-state metaphor: control surface, review desk, secure chamber, decision canvas, execution preview, proof ledger, or audit trail.
- Make the real product proof or primary workflow the most beautiful object.
- Keep brand expression subordinate to task clarity.
- Avoid turning dashboards into marketing heroes or adding decorative illustrations that do not prove the product.

### Premium Brand / Editorial

- Use an object portrait, crafted stage, material close-up, or editorial spread.
- Let typography, material, and negative space do most of the work.
- Use one rare accent for memory.
- Avoid lifestyle-photo collages, quotes, metric cards, and generic luxury beige without a real source.

## Multi-Screen Continuity

When the user asks for second and third screens, do not design independent sections. Build a scroll system.

For each screen define:

- Screen 1: emotional anchor and spatial world.
- Screen 2: collection, features, or proof that continues the same world.
- Screen 3: deeper exploration, action, archive, or conversion using the same motif language.
- Transition: one visible connector such as torn paper, shadow edge, route line, ribbon, horizon, shelf, timeline, or repeated object.
- Rhythm change: each screen should have a different job, but the same visual DNA.

Checklist:

- Does screen 2 feel like it came from below screen 1?
- Does screen 3 feel like a deeper layer, not a new template?
- Are colors, textures, motifs, typography, and spacing still from one system?
- Is the content density intentionally changing rather than randomly increasing?

## Image Prompt Block

Use this block inside image-generation prompts when the output needs strong art direction:

```text
Signature system:
Subject material: [real motifs/materials/artifacts/states]
Dominant scene: [one spatial metaphor]
Motif hierarchy: large frame [x], movement motif [y], detail motif [z], UI motif [w]
Palette source: [material/environment/product state colors]
Continuity device: [edge/ribbon/horizon/path/shelf/light trail]
Modernization ratio: [authentic source detail] vs [contemporary UI restraint]
Silence budget: keep [area] quiet so [dominant form] can breathe
```

## Failure Signs

Regenerate or redesign when:

- The page could fit any brand after changing the logo.
- Motifs are decorative stickers instead of structural composition.
- Every section uses a different visual idea.
- The second screen starts over instead of continuing the first.
- The UI sits on top of imagery instead of belonging to it.
- Cultural material becomes a collage, costume, or wallpaper.
- The first viewport has more than one dominant world.
